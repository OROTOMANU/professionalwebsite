Sustainable Road Construction in Papua New Guinea

Abstract

This thesis explores sustainable road construction practices in Papua New Guinea (PNG), a country with complex terrain, limited infrastructure, and growing demand for resilient transport systems. It evaluates materials, design strategies, and policy frameworks that promote environmental stewardship and long-term economic viability.

1. Introduction

Papua New Guinea, located in the southwestern Pacific, faces unique challenges in road development due to its mountainous topography, high rainfall, and remote communities. Despite significant investment in transport infrastructure, many roads remain unpaved or deteriorate rapidly due to poor construction standards and climate exposure.

[1] Asian Development Bank. (2020). Transport Sector Assessment, Strategy, and Road Map: Papua New Guinea.

2. Sustainable Construction Principles

  • Use of Local Materials: Promotes cost-efficiency and reduces carbon footprint.
  • Climate-Resilient Design: Incorporates drainage, slope stabilization, and erosion control.
  • Community Engagement: Ensures roads meet local needs and are maintained post-construction.
  • Lifecycle Costing: Evaluates long-term maintenance and environmental impact.

[2] World Bank. (2021). Climate-Resilient Infrastructure in PNG.

3. Case Study: Highlands Highway Rehabilitation

The Highlands Highway, a critical economic corridor, has undergone multiple upgrades to improve connectivity between Lae and Mount Hagen. Recent projects emphasize slope protection, culvert installation, and asphalt overlays using polymer-modified bitumen for durability.

[3] PNG Department of Works & Highways. (2022). Highlands Highway Upgrade Report.

4. GIS and Mapping Tools

Geographic Information Systems (GIS) are essential for planning sustainable road networks. Tools like QGIS and Google Earth allow engineers to visualize terrain, identify flood-prone zones, and optimize route alignment.

  • QGIS: Open-source platform for spatial analysis.
  • Google Earth: Useful for satellite imagery and site reconnaissance.

[4] ESRI. (2023). GIS for Infrastructure Planning.

5. Policy and Funding Landscape

PNG’s Medium Term Development Plan (MTDP IV) prioritizes sustainable infrastructure. International donors such as the ADB and World Bank support climate-adaptive road projects, while local agencies are encouraged to adopt green procurement standards.

[5] Government of Papua New Guinea. (2023). MTDP IV: 2023–2027.

6. Conclusion

Sustainable road construction in PNG requires a multidisciplinary approach—blending engineering innovation, environmental awareness, and community participation. With strategic investment and policy alignment, PNG can build resilient roads that support inclusive growth and climate adaptation.
